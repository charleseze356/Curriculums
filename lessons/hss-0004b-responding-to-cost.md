## Driver 7: Fraud, waste, and abuse

### The scale of waste — JAMA study

JAMA researchers estimate that waste accounts for **about 25% of healthcare spending in the US** — approximately **$760 billion to $935 billion each year**.

The waste breaks down into **six categories**:

1. **Failure of care coordination** — duplicated services, readmissions, care that falls through cracks between providers
2. **Fraud** — intentional deception for financial gain
3. **Administrative complexity** — the largest category; the cost of the multi-payer billing/coding/prior auth apparatus
4. **Overtreatment / low-value care** — unnecessary testing and procedures
5. **Pricing failure** — prices above what a competitive market would bear
6. **Failure of prevention** — conditions that could have been prevented but weren't, leading to expensive downstream care

The researchers note that **implementation of effective measures to eliminate waste represents an opportunity to reduce continued increases in US healthcare expenditures**. Many of these measures focus on **value-based care** — a model that delivers better care, not just more care, to improve quality and lower costs.

*Source: Berman lecture, Slide 40. Original: Berwick & Hackbarth, JAMA.*

### COVID-19 as a natural experiment on waste

The pandemic created an unprecedented natural experiment: non-COVID patients delayed or cancelled tests and treatments. In cases where **no harm was done** by delays or cancellations, medical experts can now reevaluate whether those procedures were truly necessary.

Researcher Allison Oakes noted that never before had there been such a large database to compare patients who received a particular test or treatment with those who did not. Numerous studies have shown that overtreatment causes unnecessary suffering and billions in unnecessary costs — but the pandemic provided the first large-scale evidence base for identifying which procedures can safely be deferred.

*Source: Berman lecture, Slide 39.*

## Unnecessary emergency department visits — $47 billion per year

Many Americans use the emergency room by default. Whether due to communication disconnects between insurers and patients, difficulty finding a doctor, need for after-hours care, loss of coverage, or immigration status concerns, many head to the ED even when it isn't an emergency.

This has led to at least **$47 billion in unnecessary healthcare spending** (Accenture report):

- **Those with a chronic condition were twice as likely** to have received care in the ER over the last year than people more familiar with the healthcare system
- **Low healthcare system literacy** increased from 52% in 2017 to 61% in 2021
- Those who don't know the difference between in-network and out-of-network care have low healthcare literacy

*Source: Berman lecture, Slide 33. Original: Modern Healthcare/Accenture, Sept 2021.*

**The relationship:** This connects directly to driver #3 (PCP-to-specialist ratio) and driver #5 (place of service). When primary care is weak and patients can't navigate the system, they enter through the most expensive door — the ED. The fix isn't telling patients not to go to the ED; it's building accessible primary care and system literacy.

## Unnecessary imaging — $30 billion per year

### The scale

- AHIP estimates **$30 billion per year** is "wasted" on advanced imaging
- **20–50% of advanced diagnostic procedures were unhelpful** for diagnosis
- The American College of Radiology estimates **20–30% of advanced imaging studies** are not the appropriate study, do not add anything to the diagnosis, or are repeated too soon

| Payer Group | Annual Cost of Unnecessary Imaging | Notes |
|-------------|-----------------------------------|-------|
| Medicare | $3.6 billion | Based on 47 low-value services; imaging is 4 of the top 5 overused services |
| All US Payers (National) | ~$12 billion | Includes commercial insurance, Medicaid, and private payers; CT/MRI dominate inappropriate use |

*Source: Berman lecture, Slides 35–36. Sources: AHIP, JAMA Health Forum (2025), Neiman Health Policy Institute (2025).*

### Most common low-value imaging services

| Low-Value Imaging Service | Clinical Scenario | Why It's Low-Value | Annual Waste (Medicare) |
|--------------------------|-------------------|-------------------|------------------------|
| CT/MRI for uncomplicated headache | No red flags | Does not improve outcomes; exposes patients to radiation or unnecessary follow-up | High volume driver |
| Imaging for plantar fasciitis | No trauma or systemic symptoms | Does not change management; first-line care is conservative | High volume driver |
| Imaging for syncope | No neurological deficits | Rarely identifies cause; low diagnostic yield | High volume driver |
| Early imaging for low back pain (<6 weeks) | No red flags | Increases unnecessary procedures; no benefit | High volume driver |
| Imaging for acute sinusitis | Symptoms <10 days | Does not improve care; increases cost | Moderate volume |
| Carotid imaging for dizziness | No focal neurological signs | Very low diagnostic yield | Moderate volume |

*Source: Berman lecture, Slide 37.*

**The pattern:** Every one of these scenarios has a common feature — the absence of **red flags**. The imaging doesn't change management when there are no red flags. The problem is that FFS incentives, defensive medicine, and patient expectations push physicians to image anyway. This is why reducing low-value care is a **systems problem**, not just a knowledge problem.

## How payers traditionally manage cost

Payers use a multi-layered approach to manage cost. Understanding these levers is essential because they directly shape your clinical practice — every prior authorization, every formulary restriction, every medical policy is a cost management tool.

| Lever | What It Does |
|-------|-------------|
| **Utilization Management** | Oversees whether services are medically necessary and appropriate before they're delivered |
| **Outreach and Education** | Educates providers and members on appropriate care pathways |
| **ePrior authorization / Pre-service review** | Requires approval before acute care, Rx, DME, imaging, or labs are delivered |
| **Medical Policy** | Based on peer-reviewed literature (Hayes, UTD), specialty society guidelines, and expert panels |
| **Nationally recognized guidelines** | MCG (Milliman Care Guidelines) and InterQual — evidence-based criteria for care decisions |
| **Vendor management** | Manages third-party vendors for specialized services |
| **Concurrent review** | Reviews care while the patient is in the hospital to ensure continued necessity |
| **Reconsiderations / Appeals** | Process for challenging denied services |
| **Case Management (DM/CM/ICM)** | Disease Management, Case Management, and Integrated Case Management for high-cost patients |
| **Contracting** | Negotiates rates with providers, facilities, ancillary services, imaging, labs, home health |
| **Post-service review** | Reviews claims after delivery; code rule editing (NCCI), reimbursement policy, DRG and clinical code/case validation, recoveries and cost avoidance |

*Source: Berman lecture, Slide 41.*

**The relationship:** These levers are the payer's response to the cost drivers. Prior authorization targets technology/imaging waste (driver 6). Case management targets chronic disease costs (driver 1). Contracting targets pricing failure. But each lever also creates **administrative complexity** — which is itself the largest waste category. The system is fighting itself.

## Hospital price transparency — the 8x problem

For years, patients have complained they don't know how much hospitals charge until they're billed — unlike every other commercial product. Published hospital prices now show **wide variations — up to 8x — for the same care**:

| Procedure | Low-End Price | High-End Price |
|-----------|--------------|----------------|
| Hip or knee replacement | $12,035 (Beaumont Hospital, Royal Oak) | $49,265 (DMC Harper-Hutzel, Detroit) |
| Hip or knee replacement (another hospital) | $17,448 (Henry Ford Hospital, Detroit) | $48,911 (St. Joseph Mercy Oakland, Pontiac) |
| Colonoscopy | $1,700 | $5,000+ (negotiated insurer prices) |

Only **1,225 hospitals (25% of general acute care hospitals)** disclosed negotiated prices for colonoscopies.

*Source: Berman lecture, Slides 42–43. Original: Modern Healthcare/Turquoise Health, Oct 2021.*

### Key takeaways

- **Patients are blind to hospital pricing on the front end** — unique to the healthcare industry
- **The ultimate payer is the consumer** — the patient, the American worker, and the employer that helps contribute to health coverage
- Published prices show **up to 8x variation** for the same care
- Advocates contend that **price transparency will encourage competition on prices** between hospitals, which could also lead to better patient outcomes
- "That's how every other market economy works" — without price transparency, there is no market competition on price

*Source: Berman lecture, Slide 43.*

## Exponential technologies — the Triple Aim lever

Exponential technologies are helping to drive change by making care delivery **less expensive, more efficient, and more accessible**:

- **Telemedicine** is a leading technology for real-time, convenient, private, home-based services with global expansion
- **AI** is disrupting healthcare alongside telemedicine
- Together, they aim to achieve the **Triple Aim goals**: better quality, lower cost, and an enhanced patient experience

*Source: Berman lecture, Slide 45.*

**The relationship:** Telemedicine directly addresses driver #5 (place of service) by shifting care from the ED/inpatient setting to the home. AI addresses driver #6 (technology/imaging) by improving diagnostic accuracy and reducing unnecessary studies. Both address driver #7 (waste) by reducing administrative complexity and improving care coordination.

## Medical tourism — the market response to US pricing

**Definition** (OECD): those who "travel across international borders with the intention of receiving some form of medical treatment."

- Globally, an estimated **14–16 million patients** travelled outside their home countries for treatment in 2017
- US dynamics: healthcare costs increasing, 25% of Americans un- or under-insured, **1.4 million Americans** sought care abroad in 2017

### Comparative costs

| Procedure | United States | Malaysia/India | India/Thailand |
|-----------|--------------|----------------|----------------|
| Cardiac Bypass | $123,000 | $12,100 | $7,300 |
| Angioplasty | $47,000 | $5,700 | $4,200 |

The American Journal of Medicine suggests Americans seeking cheaper healthcare will **increase by 25% every year** as they can save **30–65%** on procedure costs outside the US. Leading US medical centers (Harvard, Boston University, Johns Hopkins, Cleveland Clinic) have established hospitals and clinics outside the US.

*Source: Berman lecture, Slides 47–49.*

**The systems connection:** Medical tourism is a market response to driver #4 (specialty pharmacy) and the pricing failure waste category. When the same procedure costs 10–17x more in the US, patients will travel. This is the market attempting to correct for the absence of price transparency and centralized negotiation.

## Reimbursement models — where payment is heading

The lecture identifies a spectrum of reimbursement models that shift from volume to value:

| Model | Description | Risk |
|-------|-------------|------|
| **Value-Based Payment (VBP)** | Strategy used by purchasers to promote quality and value. Goal: shift from pure volume-based FFS to payments tied to outcomes | Baseline — the framework |
| **Shared Savings** | Entity and payer share savings if actual performance exceeds baseline/expected. May include a cap on potential savings | Upside only (entity gains if performance exceeds expectations) |
| **Partial Risk** | Entity takes upside risk — earns increased payments or bonuses for actual performance exceeding expected performance | Upside risk only |
| **Full Risk** | Entity takes upside AND downside risk — earns bonuses for exceeding expectations AND incurs penalties/reduced payments for falling below | Upside + downside risk |

*Source: Berman lecture, Slide 50.*

**The mechanism:** The spectrum progressively shifts financial risk from the payer to the provider. In pure FFS, the payer bears all the risk (paying for every service regardless of outcome). In full risk, the provider bears the risk — if they deliver inefficient, low-quality care, they lose money. This aligns incentives toward coordination, prevention, and appropriate (not maximal) care.

## Universal performance measures — how success is measured

| Measure Category | Specific Metrics |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Medication Adherence** | Proportion of Days Covered (PDC) |
| **Potentially Preventable Events** | Initial Admissions (PPA), Re-admissions (PPR), ER Visits (PPV), Ancillary Studies (PPS) |
| **Quality Measures** | HEDIS, STARS |
| **NICU days/1,000** | Neonatal intensive care utilization |
| **Financial Metrics** | Medical Cost Ratio (MCR), ALR, Margin, PMPM (per member per month) costs |

*Source: Berman lecture, Slide 51.*

**The relationship:** These measures are the feedback loop for the reimbursement models. PDC measures whether medication non-adherence (driver 2) is being addressed. PPA/PPR/PPV measure whether chronic disease (driver 1) is being managed well enough to prevent expensive acute events. HEDIS and STARS measure quality — the "better care" leg of the Triple Aim. MCR and PMPM measure cost efficiency — the "lower cost" leg.

## Expanded healthcare coverage — the structural fix

The lecture identifies **universal health insurance coverage** as a structural solution — providing coverage for all people within the US via either a **single-payer** or **pluralistic** system model.

*Source: Berman lecture, Slide 52. Reference: American College of Physicians position paper, Annals of Internal Medicine, Vol. 148(1).*

This is the most systemic of the solutions — it directly addresses the administrative complexity waste category (the largest one) by simplifying the payer landscape, and it addresses the "failure of prevention" waste category by ensuring everyone has access to primary care before conditions become expensive.

## What does this mean for you? — the physician's role

Dr. Berman closes the lecture with specific physician responsibilities. This is the translation from cost driver to clinical practice:

1. **Physician engagement is critical** — cost cannot be managed without physicians at the table
2. **Reverse imbalances in distribution of services** — PCP/specialist ratio, duplication of services, fraud/waste/abuse, SDOH, and the impact on underserved populations
3. **Address the price of labor, goods including pharmaceuticals and administrative costs** in the US
4. **Population management** — risk stratification, adjustment, with outcome-based reimbursement
5. **Achieve the Triple Aim** via Universal Performance Measures
6. **Practice to the full extent of licensure** / Inter-professional Education
7. **Integrated Care Management** by closing gaps in care (Transition of Care Checklist)
8. **Leverage technology** on personalized care — telemedicine, home-based and convenient care, early detection and intervention, along with integration and analysis of data enabled by AI with human oversight
9. **Embrace change**

*Source: Berman lecture, Slide 53.*

**The systems thinking connection:** Each of these responsibilities maps to a cost driver:

| Physician Responsibility | Cost Driver Addressed |
|--------------------------|----------------------|
| Reverse PCP/specialist imbalance | Driver 3 (PCP ratio) |
| Address pharmaceutical prices | Driver 4 (specialty pharmacy) |
| Population management / risk stratification | Driver 1 (chronic disease) |
| Integrated care management / close gaps | Driver 7 (waste — failure of care coordination) |
| Leverage telemedicine / AI | Driver 5 (place of service) + Driver 6 (technology) |
| Address SDOH impact on underserved | Driver 8 (social determinants) |
| Practice to full extent of licensure | Driver 3 (PCP ratio — maximize primary care capacity) |

This is why the next chapter — systems thinking, quality improvement, and patient safety — is the linking domain. Cost drivers are system outputs. Fixing them requires seeing the system, not just the individual decisions. The PDSA cycle, the Swiss Cheese Model, and root cause analysis are the tools you'll use to identify and fix the system conditions that produce waste.
