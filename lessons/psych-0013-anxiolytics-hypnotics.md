*This lesson zooms into treatment time horizon from the anxiety map. Place each drug by whether it builds durable control or provides immediate sedation at the cost of dependence and withdrawal risk.*

## The time-horizon rule

| Role | Examples | Main tradeoff |
|---|---|---|
| Durable control | CBT, SSRIs/SNRIs, buspirone | Delayed onset, but suited to chronic treatment |
| Rapid symptom relief | Benzodiazepines | Fast effect, but tolerance, dependence, sedation, and withdrawal |
| Performance-only symptoms | Propranolol | Targets autonomic symptoms; avoid when beta-blockade is unsafe |
| Sleep onset support | Z-drugs and selected hypnotics | Sedation and dependence/behavior risks require caution |

*Sources: FA Clinical Algorithms, Ch. 27-11, pp. 796–800; FA Step 2 CK 8E, Psychiatry pp. 394–397.*

## Benzodiazepine safety is the testable relationship

Benzodiazepines increase GABA-A chloride-channel opening frequency. They can treat acute anxiety, agitation, panic, alcohol withdrawal, and catatonia, but long-term routine use works against the treatment goal by producing physiologic dependence.

Intoxication is often managed supportively with respiratory monitoring. Withdrawal is the more dangerous side of the pair: taper with a long-acting benzodiazepine rather than stopping abruptly.
