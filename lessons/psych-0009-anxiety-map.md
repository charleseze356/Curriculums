*This is the map lesson for anxiety, trauma, and OCD. Every subsequent lesson in this chapter hangs off the framework established here: rule out source → identify trigger/pattern → apply duration → choose targeted CBT and medication.*

## The sorting frame

| Dominant organizing clue | Main branch |
|---|---|
| Uncontrollable worry across domains | GAD |
| Recurrent unexpected attacks + anticipatory fear | Panic disorder ± agoraphobia |
| Fear tied to scrutiny or a specific object/situation | Social anxiety or specific phobia |
| Intrusive thoughts and/or anxiety-reducing rituals | OCD |
| Trauma followed by intrusion, avoidance, negative mood/cognition, hyperarousal | Acute stress disorder or PTSD |
| Distress after an identifiable stressor without another full syndrome | Adjustment disorder |

*Sources: FA Step 2 CK 8E, Psychiatry pp. 394–397; FA Clinical Algorithms, Ch. 27-10, Fig. 27.10, pp. 794–796.*

## Why this chapter is early

The NBOME COMAT blueprint assigns **20–25%** to anxiety, trauma/stressor-related, dissociative, and OCD-related disorders. The map is therefore load-bearing: it turns many similar-looking complaints into a small number of discriminating axes.

## The universal gate

Before naming a primary anxiety disorder, establish significant impairment and rule out medical and substance/medication causes. Panic-like symptoms can arise from cardiac disease, hyperthyroidism, pheochromocytoma, intoxication, or withdrawal.

## Treatment architecture

CBT and SSRI/SNRI therapy recur across the map, but the mechanism matters: exposure for phobias and panic, exposure and response prevention for OCD, and trauma-focused approaches for PTSD. Benzodiazepines offer short-term symptom relief but carry tolerance, dependence, and withdrawal risk.
