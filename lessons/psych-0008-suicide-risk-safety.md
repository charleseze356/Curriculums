*This lesson zooms into safety from the mood and interview maps. Place it after mood disorders because depressive and bipolar presentations often become clinically urgent through suicide risk.*

## The core frame

FA organizes suicidality around **acute/chronic risk vs protective factors** and asks whether there is a suicide plan and intent.

*Source: FA Step 2 CK 8E, Psychiatry Ch. 27-17, Fig. 27.17.*

---

## What to assess

| Domain | Examples from FA |
|---|---|
| Acute risk | Acute mental-disorder symptoms, hopelessness, impulsivity, recent loss, anniversary of loss, preparation for suicide |
| Chronic risk | Male sex, age <19 or >45, depression/mental illness, previous attempts, ethanol/substance use, psychosis, separation/divorce/widowhood, serious prior attempt, no support, stated future intent, chronic/terminal illness |
| Protective factors | Coping skills, religion/cultural beliefs, social support, mental health connection, limited lethal means |
| Lethal means | Firearms, sharp objects, overdose medications, hanging supplies |

---

## Level-of-care logic

| Presentation | FA management direction |
|---|---|
| Active plan + intent | High acute risk; hospitalize/stabilize; remove lethal means |
| Active thoughts without intent/plan | Intermediate acute risk may fit; consider partial hospitalization/day program depending risk/protection |
| Passive death wish with severe risk context | Can still be high acute risk; inpatient hospitalization may be needed |

---

## Source gap: homicidal ideation risk

The COMAT learner objectives explicitly include risk assessment for suicide and homicidal ideation. FA Ch. 27-17 gives a usable suicide framework but does not provide an equivalent homicidal-ideation framework in the psychiatry section. That item remains flagged for a COMAT/clerkship supplement rather than invented.
