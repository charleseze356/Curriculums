## Why this lesson exists

Medical school teaches two sciences: **basic science** (how the body works) and **clinical science** (how to diagnose and treat disease). For most of the 20th century, that was considered sufficient.

It isn't. Physicians today practice inside complex, interconnected systems — insurance structures, hospital hierarchies, care teams, electronic records, population health programs — that powerfully shape patient outcomes independent of what any individual clinician does. A physician who understands disease but not the system around disease cannot fully serve patients.

Health Systems Science (HSS) is the **third medical science**: the study of how care is organized, delivered, and improved.

*Source: Skochelak et al., Health Systems Science, 2nd Ed., Ch. 1 — "What Is Health Systems Science? Building an Integrated Vision"*

---

## The three sciences of medicine

| Science | Question it answers |
|---|---|
| Basic science | How does the body work? |
| Clinical science | How do I diagnose and treat this patient? |
| **Health systems science** | **How is care organized, delivered, and improved?** |

These are not competing — they are complementary. A complete physician needs all three.

## The HSS curricular domains

The textbook organizes HSS into three rings (Fig. 1.6):

**Core functional domains** (inner ring) — the domains physicians directly apply in practice:
- Health care structures and processes
- Health care policy and economics
- Clinical informatics
- Value in health care
- Population health and social determinants

**Foundational domains** (middle ring) — the enabling knowledge:
- Patient safety
- Quality improvement
- Teamwork and interprofessional practice
- Leadership
- Health law and ethics

**Linking domain** — **Systems thinking** connects all of these. It is the cognitive habit that lets a physician see a problem not just as an individual failure but as an output of a system — and therefore fixable at the system level.

*Source: Skochelak et al., Health Systems Science, 2nd Ed., Ch. 1, Section V — "Health systems science curricular domains" and Fig. 1.6*

## Why it matters for your training

HSS is not administrative overhead. It is what allows you to:

- Understand why a patient's care is fragmented across providers
- Recognize when a medical error is a system failure, not just a human one
- Speak the language of value, quality metrics, and population health that hospital systems and insurers use
- Function effectively in care teams you don't lead

Your syllabus frames this as clinician readiness: the healthcare environment is changing faster than individual training can track. HSS is what bridges that gap.
