*This lesson zooms into depressive presentations from the mood map. Place it after the mood map because the same depressive symptoms can mean MDD, adjustment, grief, persistent depressive disorder, medical disease, or bipolar depression.*

## MDD as a syndrome

FA defines a major depressive episode by low mood and SIGECAPS symptoms for at least 2 weeks. The clinical point is not the mnemonic alone; it is that depression becomes a syndrome when symptoms cluster, persist, impair function, and create safety risk.

*Source: FA Step 2 CK 8E, Psychiatry Ch. 27-7 and 27-8.*

---

## Initial management frame

FA's MDD management begins with:

1. **Risk assessment**
2. **Psychotherapy**
3. **Pharmacologic treatment**: SSRI/SNRI, bupropion, mirtazapine, then TCA/MAOI options if needed
4. **Treatment-refractory options**: augmentation, TMS, ketamine, ECT

This order matters. If the patient is actively suicidal, psychotic, or medically unsafe, choosing the outpatient antidepressant is not the next best step.

---

## Antidepressant selection as matching, not memorizing

| Clinical issue | FA-supported medication logic |
|---|---|
| First-line unipolar depression/anxiety | SSRI/SNRI + CBT |
| Weight gain, fatigue, sexual side effects | Consider bupropion, unless seizure/eating disorder risk |
| Underweight or insomnia | Consider mirtazapine |
| Chronic pain/fibromyalgia/neuropathic pain | SNRI can fit |
| QRS widening from TCA overdose | Sodium bicarbonate if QRS >100 ms, hypotensive, or arrhythmic |
| Atypical/treatment-resistant depression | MAOI may fit, but tyramine/serotonin-syndrome risks matter |

---

## What to carry forward

MDD is common, but the exam usually asks for the **next move**: assess risk, rule out bipolar/medical/substance causes, choose a treatment matched to the patient's constraints, or escalate when safety demands it.
