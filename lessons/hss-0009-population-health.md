*This is the map lesson for Chapter 5. It zooms out from the individual patient encounter (the focus of Chapters 1-4) to the population perspective. Every subsequent concept in population health — SDOH, transitions of care, data analytics — hangs off the framework established here: the shift from treating disease to preventing it, from individual authority to team-based coordination, from volume to value.*

## Why this lesson exists

Everything in HSS so far has been about the system: its structure (Ch 1), its economics (Ch 2), its quality and safety mechanisms (Ch 3), and the physician's role as leader and advocate (Ch 4). But all of that operates at the level of the individual encounter — one patient, one visit, one diagnosis.

Population health is a different cognitive frame. It asks: what if the unit of care isn't the patient, but the population? What if the goal isn't treating disease but preventing it? What if the physician's job isn't to be the authority but to lead a team that keeps people healthy?

This shift is not theoretical — it's the direction the entire US healthcare system is moving. Value-based care (hss-0008) is the payment mechanism. Population health is the clinical practice.

*Source: Wehbe A. "Population Health." Rowan-Virtua SOM HSS II Lecture, July 2025. Slides 1–32.*

## The definition

Population health has three components:

1. **Distribution of health outcomes** (or status) in a population
2. **Determinants** (drivers) of those health outcomes
3. **Policies & interventions** that impact determinants

The operational definition: "A systematic approach to health care that aims to prevent and cure disease by keeping people healthy."

This means:
- Connecting prevention, wellness, and behavioral health science with health care delivery, quality, safety, disease prevention/management, and economics
- Identifying socioeconomic and cultural factors
- Modeling disease states, mapping incidence, predicting impact
- Using data analysis to design social and community interventions and new models of care delivery that stress care coordination and ease of access

*Source: Wehbe lecture, Slides 7–8. School of Population Health definition.*

## How population health differs from conventional care

| Dimension | Conventional Healthcare | Population Health |
|---|---|---|
| **Goal** | Diagnose, treat, cure | Prevent disease |
| **Focus** | Cure disease | Emphasize wellness |
| **Expertise** | Physician's expertise | Coordinated care / Medical home |
| **Payment** | Fee-for-service | Global / Quality payments |
| **Patient role** | Limited patient contact | Patient accountability |
| **Information** | Lack of access | Shared health information |
| **Physician role** | Doctor as center of authority | Doctor as center of care team |

**The key shift:** The physician moves from "center of authority" (every decision flows through me) to "center of care team" (I lead a team that manages the patient's whole health). This is the same shift Dr. Sood described in the Bringing it All Together lecture (hss-0007) — but here it's driven by the payment model, not just leadership philosophy.

*Source: Wehbe lecture, Slide 11.*

## What counts as a "population"?

A population is any defined group:

- **Residents of a geographic region** (e.g., South Jersey)
- **A company's employees** (employer-sponsored population)
- **Age-based** (e.g., all adults 65+)
- **A primary care physician's patients** (panel-based)
- **A class of patients with specific chronic disease(s)** (e.g., all CKD Stage 4/5 patients)

**Why this matters:** The population definition determines what interventions work. A geographic population needs community-level interventions (zoning, transportation, food access). A disease-specific population needs clinical care coordination (medication management, specialist coordination, disease monitoring). The Strive Health model (hss-0008) is a disease-specific population (CKD/ESRD); a public health department is a geographic population.

*Source: Wehbe lecture, Slide 9.*

## Social Determinants of Health (SDOH)

The five SDOH factors:

1. **Education access** — health literacy, understanding of conditions and treatments
2. **Health access** — insurance coverage, provider availability, transportation
3. **Housing insecurity** — stable housing as a prerequisite for health management
4. **Community support** — social isolation drives poor outcomes; community networks protect
5. **Economic stability** — income determines food access, medication adherence, stress levels

**Why SDOH is clinical, not social:** Under conventional care, SDOH is "not my problem" — the physician treats the disease and refers to social services. Under population health, SDOH IS the clinical problem — because the practice bears the cost of poor outcomes. A patient who is food insecure will not control their diabetes. A patient who is housing insecure will cycle through the ED. These are not side issues; they are the upstream drivers of the downstream costs.

**The connection to cost drivers (hss-0004):** SDOH is driver #8 — the upstream cause of chronic disease (driver #1), reversible illness (driver #2), and ED utilization (driver #5). You cannot reduce cost without addressing SDOH. Population health makes this financially viable because prevention is savings under VBC.

*Source: Wehbe lecture, Slides 15, 29.*

## Transitions of care: the critical handoff

### The problem

The most dangerous moment in a patient's care is the transition — hospital to home, hospital to SNF, specialist to PCP. This is where information is lost, medications are mismatched, follow-up falls through, and readmissions happen.

### The transitions of care team

**Pre-discharge team:**
- Case managers, social workers, nurse practitioners
- Measuring each patient's post-hospital health risks
- Guiding post-hospital care:
  - Patient education content (electronically shared)
  - Contact center
  - Patient portal
  - Care coordination

**Post-hospital follow-up:**
- Primary care offices with access on nights & weekends
- Specialty care offices with open visit slots reserved for newly discharged patients
- Behavioral health integration
- Extended care practice: nursing homes, assisted living facilities, home visits, telemedicine

**The transfer of care responsibility:** This is the critical handoff. The hospitalist team transfers responsibility to the PCP, specialists, and extended care facilities. If this handoff is uncoordinated, the patient falls through the cracks — missed medications, missed follow-up, readmission.

*Source: Wehbe lecture, Slides 19–22.*

## Metrics, data, and AI

Population health is data-driven. You cannot manage what you cannot measure.

**What gets measured:**
- Disease incidence and prevalence in the population
- Utilization patterns (ED visits, hospitalizations, readmissions)
- Quality metrics (HEDIS for Medicaid, Stars for Medicare — both from NCQA)
- Cost per patient per period
- SDOH screening results
- Care gap closure (missing screenings, missing vaccinations, missing follow-up)

**AI and machine learning:**
- Predictive modeling: identify patients at risk of deterioration before they crash
- Risk stratification: red/yellow/green stoplight model (from Berman lecture, hss-0003)
- Automated care gap identification
- Population-level trend analysis

**The connection to VBC (hss-0008):** Strive Health used data to identify CKD Stage 4/5 patients before they crashed into dialysis. That's population health + AI + VBC in one model — data identifies the risk, the team intervenes, VBC pays for the prevention.

*Source: Wehbe lecture, Slides 23–25.*

## How to make the shift

Dr. Wehbe identifies the steps to shift from conventional to population health:

1. **Practice based on evidence** — not anecdote
2. **Reduce unexplained clinical variation** — standardize care pathways
3. **Continuously measure and feedback loop** — close the loop on quality data
4. **Engage with patients across the continuum of care** — not just the acute episode
5. **Digital health / virtual first** — technology extends reach
6. **Change the culture** — from "doctor as authority" to "doctor as team leader"

**The three requirements for population health to be real (Slide 27):**
1. Attribute real people to distinct populations
2. Influence & measure the health status of these people individually and collectively over time
3. Negotiate contracts to achieve long-term financial sustainability for this strategy

Without all three, population health is "a purely academic exercise."

*Source: Wehbe lecture, Slides 16, 27.*

## How this connects

| Prior concept | Connection |
|---|---|
| **What is HSS (hss-0001)** | Population health is one of the three pillars of HSS (alongside basic and clinical science). It's the domain that asks "why does this population have these outcomes?" |
| **Cost drivers (hss-0004)** | SDOH is driver #8 — the upstream cause. Population health addresses SDOH directly, which reduces drivers #1 (chronic disease), #2 (reversible illness), and #5 (place of service). |
| **Value-based care (hss-0008)** | VBC is the payment mechanism that makes population health financially sustainable. Without VBC, prevention is a cost. With VBC, prevention is savings. |
| **Systems thinking (hss-0005)** | Population health IS systems thinking applied to health — it sees outcomes as system outputs, not individual failures. |
| **Teams (hss-0007)** | Population health requires team-based care. The physician cannot manage a population alone — the team extends reach. |
| **Integrated care (hss-0007)** | Population health solves care fragmentation through integrated, coordinated care across the continuum. |

**The through-line:** Population health is where everything converges. The cost drivers explain why the system is expensive. VBC explains how payment can change behavior. Systems thinking explains how to see the problems. Teams explain how to execute. Population health is the frame that ties them all together — because the unit of care is the population, and every tool in HSS serves that unit.
