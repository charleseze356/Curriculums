## The frame: privilege, power, and obligation

Sood opens with two definitions that reframe what it means to be a physician:

| Concept | Definition | The obligation |
|---|---|---|
| **Privilege** | A special right, advantage, or immunity granted or available only to a particular person or group | You have access, knowledge, and social authority most people don't. The question is: what is possible with your privilege and power? |
| **Power** | The capacity or ability to direct or influence the behavior of others or the course of events | You can shape systems. Not using that power is a choice — and it's a choice that leaves broken systems broken. |

This connects directly to the advocacy lesson (hss-0006): advocacy is the *action* that flows from recognizing your privilege and power. This lesson is about the *skills and structures* that make that action effective.

*Source: Sood lecture, Slides 3–4.*

## Physicians as systems designers

Sood highlights three physicians who designed new systems rather than just practicing within existing ones:

- **Dr. Rushika Fernandopulle** — redesigned primary care delivery (Iora Health)
- **Dr. Jeffrey Brenner** — used data mapping to target high-utilizer patients (Camden Coalition)
- **Dr. Tom Lee** — physician-executive bridging clinical practice and health technology

### Barriers to physicians as systems designers

| Barrier | What it means | How to overcome it |
|---|---|---|
| **Risk aversion** | Medical training rewards avoiding failure; systems design requires experimentation | Build creative confidence: tinker, fail, try again. Define fears instead of goals (fear-setting exercise). |
| **Expertise/knowledge gaps** | Medical training doesn't include design, finance, informatics, policy | Learn adjacent languages: human-centered design, finance, public health, informatics, legal, admin |
| **Limited culture of systems design** | Medicine doesn't have a strong tradition of physician-builders | Put yourself in "superlinear" environments — cities, networks, conferences where idea generation scales faster than population |

### How to get started

- **Network with peers** — inside and outside medicine
- **Intern with diverse organizations** — startups, venture capital, government, nonprofits
- **Advising/consulting** — bring clinical expertise to non-clinical organizations
- **Acquire other skills** — from tech, public health, finance, computer science
- **Conferences** — cross-disciplinary exposure
- **70/30 practice/innovation balance** — spend 30% of your career on innovation, not just clinical practice

*Source: Sood lecture, Slides 5–9, 19–20.*

## Why teamwork matters: high-reliability organizations

### The case study: aviation and the military

In organizations where human endeavor involves risk and substantial consequences for failure (aviation, military), **teamwork** can:
- Lessen risk
- Improve rate of success

A pilot is always assisted by a co-pilot and guided by air-traffic controllers and ground crew. **Does this concept apply to healthcare?** Yes — and the evidence is stark.

### To Err Is Human (IOM, 1999)

The landmark Institute of Medicine report that made teamwork a national priority:

- **98,000 preventable deaths** occurred in the US annually due to medical error
- **Recommendation:** Establish interdisciplinary team training programs (simulation, team management)

### Why failures happen

Research shows that failures are rarely the result of a single individual — they are the result of **collective systems failures**. At the root is often poor team performance:

- Communication failure
- Poor interpersonal interaction
- Lack of understanding roles

This is the same systems thinking principle from hss-0005: **blame the system, not the person.** But here the system is the *team*, not just the workflow.

*Source: Sood lecture, Slides 22–29.*

## What makes a team effective

A team is effective when it consistently achieves its goals. In healthcare, the primary goals are:
- Improve the quality of care delivered to the patient
- Improve the patient's health

### Four characteristics of high-performing teams

1. **Shared understanding** of goals and a shared perception of the situation
2. **Clear team structure** — tasks and roles aligned with members' strengths
3. **Open, consistent, concise, frequent, and planned communication**
4. **Mutual respect, coordinated efforts, and diverse backgrounds**

### What happens when these are absent

Two cases from the lecture illustrate the consequences:

**Case 1 — Restraints and the missing family member:** A patient in restraints (to prevent pulling his tracheostomy tube) is given a break when his wife visits. The wife leaves the room without telling staff. The patient is now at risk. **Lesson:** Family members are part of the healthcare team. A patient-centered approach includes the family in understanding the need for restraints and their role in safety. Communicate with all team members — including family.

**Case 2 — The delayed Code Blue:** A patient in cardiac arrest — but the team hesitates to call a Code Blue because they're uncertain about the patient's healthcare directive. **Lesson:** Resuscitation status must be clear at the start of every shift, communicated during morning huddle, and visibly printed on a white board. Any delay negatively impacts time-sensitive care. Hesitation kills.

*Source: Sood lecture, Slides 30–34.*

## Constructing and developing teams

### Constructing a team

1. **Select personnel** — members must possess the knowledge, skills, and attitudes to accomplish the goal
2. **Choose appropriate size** — influences communication patterns, ability to meet, assign work, and redirect activity
3. **Define operational rules and communication tools**

### Stages of team development

| Stage | What happens | Medical student parallel |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Forming** | Members identified, goals set, capabilities understood | First day of clerkship — focus shifts from student-as-learner to team-member |
| **2. Storming** | Roles delineated, communication patterns established, tension and competitiveness | Joining a team tested by difficult situations, or one that just lost a key member |
| **3. Norming** | Trust established, reliance on each other, open disagreement with mutual respect | Team can disagree productively without breaking down |
| **4. Performing** | Mature team, shared goals, trust is norm, productive and efficient | High-functioning clinical team that delivers care smoothly |

**Why you can't skip Storming:** Trust hasn't been built. The first real conflict will derail the team because there's no mechanism for handling disagreement. Storming is where those mechanisms get built. Norming is where they get tested. Only then can the team perform.

*Source: Sood lecture, Slides 35–41.*

## Interprofessional core competencies

The five competencies for interprofessional team practice (IPEC):

| Competency | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Provide patient-centered care** | Identify, respect, and care about patients' differences, values, preferences, and needs; coordinate continuous care; share decision making; advocate disease prevention and wellness |
| **Employ evidence-based practice** | Integrate research with clinical expertise and patient values for optimum care |
| **Apply quality improvement** | Identify errors and hazards; implement safety design principles (standardization, simplification); measure quality in structure, process, and outcomes; design and test interventions |
| **Utilize informatics** | Communicate, manage knowledge, mitigate error, and support decision making using information technology |
| **Work in interprofessional teams** | Cooperate, collaborate, communicate, and integrate care to ensure continuity and reliability |

**The WHO connection:** Once you understand how to work interprofessionally, you are ready to enter the workplace as a member of a collaborative practice team.

*Source: Sood lecture, Slides 42–48.*

## Care fragmentation → integrated care

### The problem

**Care fragmentation** occurs when the delivery of healthcare is spread across an excessively large number of poorly coordinated components, between which synergy is lacking. It is a potentially important source of inefficiency in the US health care system.

During a 3-day hospital stay, a patient may interact with as many as **30 different professionals** — physicians, nurses, X-ray and laboratory technicians, nutritional staff, and transport staff. If these professionals don't communicate effectively, care fragments.

### The solution: integrated care

> "The care a patient experiences as a result of a team of primary care and behavioral health clinicians working together with patients and families, using a systematic, cost-effective, and evidence-based approach to provide patient-centered and holistic care to a population."

**Common applications:**
- Integrating behavioral health and primary care (aligned EHRs, locations, goals, philosophies)
- Integrated teams for substance use disorder treatment (aligned payment structures)
- Teams helping patients navigate insurance coverage for medications

**Benefits:**
- Penetration of services into population
- Access to care for marginalized populations
- Improved detection of implicit bias (especially anchoring and confirmation bias)

*Source: Sood lecture, Slides 49–54.*
