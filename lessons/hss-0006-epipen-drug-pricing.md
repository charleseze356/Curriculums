*This lesson zooms into a specific case study — the EpiPen pricing scandal — that connects the cost drivers from Chapter 2 to the advocacy and leadership themes of Chapter 4. Place it in the map as the bridge: you now understand why the system is expensive; this lesson shows what happens when a single drug exemplifies the entire mechanism, and what the physician's professional obligation is in response.*

## Why this lesson exists

You've already learned the eight drivers of healthcare cost (Lesson hss-0004). Specialty pharmacy is driver #4. But understanding a cost driver in the abstract is different from seeing it operate on a single drug, a single company, and a single patient population.

The EpiPen case is the microcosm. It takes every structural problem in US drug pricing — market exclusivity, patent thickets, regulatory capture, mandatory coverage laws — and distills them into one story you can hold in your hand. A device that costs pennies to make, sold for $100, then $600, by a company with a virtual monopoly, to patients who will die without it.

This lecture also introduces **physician advocacy** — the professional obligation to act when you identify a threat to patient health through your clinical work. This is not optional. The AOA and AMA both codify it.

*Source: Hurst DJ. "The EpiPen Drug Pricing Scandal." Rowan-Virtua SOM HSS II Lecture, June 2026. Slides 1–25.*

## The mechanism: why US drug prices are structurally high

Three structural facts explain why the US pays more for drugs than any other developed nation:

1. **No price caps during market exclusivity.** The US allows brand-name manufacturers to charge whatever they want during their government-granted market exclusivity period. No other developed nation permits this.

2. **Mandatory coverage laws.** Numerous laws and rules require government or private payors to cover many high-priced drugs. Manufacturers face no downward price pressure because payors must pay.

3. **Patent thickets.** Companies build webs of patents designed to delay generic entry and extend exclusivity as long as possible. The drug doesn't improve — the legal protection just extends.

The result: brand-name drugs make up only ~10% of prescriptions but account for ~75% of spending. And the most concerning examples are drugs available for decades — insulin, naloxone, epinephrine — where the active ingredient is cheap and old but the price has been inflated through market power.

*Source: Hurst lecture, Slides 6–7. Kesselheim et al., JAMA 2016;316(8):858–871.*

## The case: EpiPen

### The timeline

- **1988:** EpiPen introduced. Active ingredient (epinephrine) has been around even longer. Costs pennies to make.
- **2007:** Mylan acquires EpiPen. At this point, two EpiPens cost ~$100.
- **2007–2016:** Mylan raises the price repeatedly. By 2016, two EpiPens cost more than $600.
- **The business model:** Find an old, cheap drug with virtually no competition. Raise the price over and over as high as you can. This is the same model Martin Shkreli used with Daraprim and Valeant used with multiple drugs.

### Why this is possible

Mylan had a **virtual monopoly** over the epinephrine auto-injector market. With no generic competitor and mandatory coverage by payors, there was no market force pushing prices down. The FDA didn't approve the first generic EpiPen until August 2018 — more than a decade after Mylan acquired the product.

*Source: Hurst lecture, Slides 15, 25. Rep. Elijah Cummings, Congressional testimony.*

## The impact: what high drug prices do to patients

This is not abstract. The consequences are measurable:

| Consequence | Data |
|---|---|
| **Non-adherence** | ~25% of respondents in a 2015 Kaiser poll didn't fill a prescription in the last year because of cost |
| **Worse outcomes** | Patients prescribed costly branded products adhere worse and have worse health outcomes than those receiving generics |
| **Avoidable costs** | Non-adherence from all causes contributes ~$105 billion in avoidable healthcare costs annually |
| **Increased mortality** | Non-adherence to important medications directly increases patient death |
| **Insurance erosion** | Drug costs passed through premium increases make insurance less affordable, forcing people off plans |
| **Spillover** | Medicaid programs have cut coverage for other services to absorb expanding drug budgets |

*Source: Hurst lecture, Slides 11–13. Kesselheim et al., JAMA 2016.*

**The systems connection:** This is the same mechanism from the cost drivers lesson — non-adherence feeds back into chronic disease progression, which feeds ED visits and hospitalizations, which feeds the $4.5T in total spending. Drug pricing is not a separate problem; it is a node in the cost system.

## The response: physician advocacy

### What advocacy is

Two definitions from the literature:

> "Action by a physician to promote those social, economic, educational, and political changes that ameliorate the suffering and threats to human health and well-being that he or she identifies through his or her professional work and expertise."
> — Earnest, Wong, Federico. *Acad Med.* 2010;85(1):63-67.

> "Purposeful actions by health professionals to address determinants of health which negatively impact individuals and communities by either informing those who can enact change by initiating, mobilizing, and organizing activities to make change happen, with or on behalf of the individuals or communities with whom the health professionals work."
> — Oandasan. *Acad Med.* 2005;80(10 suppl):38-41.

The common thread: advocacy is **purposeful action** driven by what you identify through your clinical work. You see the harm; you act on it.

### The professional obligation

This is codified, not aspirational:

- **AOA Rules and Guidelines on Physicians' Professional Conduct:** "A physician has an obligation to pursue a patient's best interests and to be an advocate for the patient. In so doing, physicians shall conduct themselves in a civil manner."
- **AMA Declaration of Professional Responsibility:** Physicians should "Advocate for social, economic, educational, and political changes that ameliorate suffering and contribute to human well-being."

### The spectrum of advocacy

Advocacy is not just lobbying Congress. It operates across a spectrum:

1. **Medical society affiliation** — joining and participating in professional organizations that advocate on behalf of patients and the profession
2. **State/national elected representatives** — contacting legislators, providing expert testimony, supporting or opposing legislation
3. **Practice management** — making cost-conscious prescribing decisions in your own practice, choosing generics when appropriate
4. **Community education** — educating patients and communities about medication costs, alternatives, and resources
5. **Policy advocate** — engaging in systemic policy change, from drug pricing reform to insurance coverage expansion

*Source: Hurst lecture, Slides 19–24. Earnest et al., 2010.*
