*This lesson zooms into the first branch from the clerkship map. Place it before every disorder-specific lesson: the diagnosis is unsafe until the symptom source is located.*

## The first branch

FA's psychiatry chapter begins with a psychiatric complaint split into three source categories:

| Source | What it means clinically |
|---|---|
| Primary psychiatric | The syndrome itself is the main disease process. |
| Substance-induced | Intoxication, withdrawal, or medication exposure explains the syndrome. |
| Medical | Neurologic, endocrine, infectious, metabolic, cardiopulmonary, hepatic/renal, autoimmune, nutritional, or sleep-related disease is driving symptoms. |

*Source: FA Step 2 CK 8E, Psychiatry Ch. 27-1, Fig. 27.1.*

---

## Why this branch matters

Psychiatric symptoms are not specific to psychiatric disease. FA explicitly flags neurologic disease, endocrinopathies, infection, electrolyte/uremic/hepatic disease, lupus, iron deficiency, and OSA as medical causes that can present psychiatrically.

The clinical move is: **do not reward the most psychologically vivid symptom with the diagnosis.** Hallucinations, anxiety, mood change, and agitation can be downstream outputs of other disease.

---

## Substance-induced clues

FA highlights two high-yield clues:

- **Timing:** symptoms develop during or soon after substance exposure or withdrawal.
- **Organic features:** visual/tactile hallucinations, autonomic findings, abnormal pupils, and skin findings.

The workup can include urine toxicology, blood alcohol level when useful, drug history, pupil exam, and skin exam.

---

## The practical rule

Before you name a primary disorder, say the rule-out sentence:

> "This looks psychiatric, but I first need to consider substance-induced and medical causes because those change both diagnosis and immediate management."

That one sentence prevents most psychiatry differential errors.
