    a: "FFS pays per service rendered — more services = more revenue, incentivizing overutilization. ## Why this lesson exists

You've learned the eight cost drivers and the $760-935B in annual waste. But understanding waste is different from understanding the alternative. If FFS creates the incentive to overutilize, what replaces it?

Value-based care is the answer — and it's not theoretical. Dr. Modarai (former Chief Clinical Officer at Strive Health) presents a working model that reduced hospitalizations 42% and saved 20% total cost of care for kidney patients. This lesson covers the mechanism, the payment models, and the case study.

*Source: Modarai F. "Value Based Care: Reimagining How Care is Delivered." Rowan-Virtua SOM HSS II Lecture, June 2026. Slides 1–31.*

## The frame: Health > Healthcare

Dr. Modarai opens with a stark data point: a **33-year difference in life expectancy** between people in Barry Farms and Friendship Heights — two DC neighborhoods 10 miles apart.

This is the core argument for VBC: healthcare (the services we deliver) is not the same as health (the outcomes that matter). Under FFS, we maximize healthcare delivery. Under VBC, we maximize health — which often means delivering *less* healthcare (fewer hospitalizations, fewer unnecessary procedures) and investing more in prevention, coordination, and social determinants.

**The cycling problem:** Many people with complex needs repeatedly cycle through multiple systems (ED, hospital, specialist, PCP) without deriving lasting benefit. This cycling causes stress on patients, providers, and systems. Under FFS, each cycle generates revenue. Under VBC, each cycle is a cost failure.

*Source: Modarai lecture, Slides 4, 9.*

## Payment models: the mechanism that changes behavior

### The three risk levels

| Model | Risk | Upside | Downside | What it incentivizes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Shared savings** | Upside only | Practice earns a split of savings | None | Reduce unnecessary utilization — but no penalty if you don't |
| **Partial risk** | Some downside | Higher upside share | Bonus reduced if targets missed | Balance quality and cost — moderate incentive |
| **Full risk** | Both | Can earn significantly more | Can owe money back | Strongest incentive to manage population health |

**The key insight:** The more downside risk a practice bears, the stronger the incentive to keep patients healthy. In shared savings, the worst case is you don't earn a bonus. In full risk, the worst case is you lose money. Full risk is where VBC becomes real — but it requires the infrastructure to manage populations, not just treat patients.

*Source: Modarai lecture, Slide 14.*

## The case study: Strive Health kidney care

### Why kidney disease?

| Statistic | Value |
|---|---|
| Americans with kidney disease | 37 million (15% of adults) |
| Annual spend on kidney disease | $420 billion |
| Medicare spend on kidney patients | 24% |
| Patients who crash into dialysis | ~70% |
| Cost difference: crash vs. planned dialysis start | $60,000 more in first year |
| African American representation in kidney failure | 35% (vs 13% of US population) |
| African American risk of kidney failure | 3x national average |

**The disparity connection:** Kidney disease disproportionately affects African Americans and Hispanics/Latinos — 3x and 1.3x more likely to have kidney failure, respectively. This is not a biological fact; it's a system output driven by SDOH, access barriers, and delayed diagnosis.

*Source: Modarai lecture, Slide 16.*

### The Strive model

**Structure:** NP-led care teams operating from Care Hubs in markets with patient density.

**Team composition:**
- Nurse Practitioner & PCP
- Medical Assistant
- Pharmacist
- Registered Nurse
- RN Care Manager
- Care Coordinator
- Social Worker
- Dietitian

**Care delivery:**
- Annual wellness visits
- Diagnosing, treating, referring, documenting for ALL conditions (not just kidney)
- Urgent care (medication refills, wound care, nail clippings)
- Home visits, phone & telehealth visits, standalone/co-located clinics
- Care coordination across nephrologist, PCP, dialysis center, health system, transplant center, vascular surgeon
- SDOH & behavioral health
- Patient empowerment & education
- Medication management

**Cadence:** Daily huddles, weekly planning, monthly reviews.

*Source: Modarai lecture, Slides 18–20.*

### Results

| Metric | Strive | Market | Improvement |
|---|---|---|---|
| Total cost of care savings | 20% | — | — |
| Hospital admissions (per thousand) | 464 | 917 | 72% reduction |
| ED visits (per thousand) | 472 | 637 | 28% reduction |
| Hospital readmissions | 20% | 28% | — |
| Home dialysis adoption | 23% | 7% | — |
| Optimal dialysis starts | 58% | 29% | +100% |
| Diabetes controlled | 49% | 26% | +77% |
| Hypertension controlled | 68% | 52% | +31% |
| eGFR decline (≥40%) | 3.2% | 6.5% | 51% reduction |

**The mechanism:** Under FFS, a dialysis crash is revenue (ED visit, hospitalization, emergent dialysis initiation). Under VBC, preventing the crash is savings. Strive identifies patients before they crash, coordinates vascular access placement, educates on home dialysis options, and manages comorbidities — all of which are costs under FFS but savings under VBC.

*Source: Modarai lecture, Slides 21–23.*

## The CMS Kidney Care Choices (KCC) Model

CMS launched the KCC model to transform kidney care delivery:

**Attribution & eligibility:**
- Nephrologists, transplant providers, and other providers (optional)
- Traditional Part A and Part B Medicare beneficiaries
- CKD Stage 4/5 & ESRD patients
- Attribution tied to patient visits with a participating nephrologist

**Payment mechanics:**
- CKD capitated payment
- Home dialysis bonus
- Kidney transplant bonus
- Shared savings APM bonus
- CMS withholds % of financial benchmark that can be earned back based on quality performance

**Quality measures:**
- Depression screening & remission
- Patient activation
- Delay of disease progression
- Optimal dialysis starts
- Mortality rate

**Scale:** 282,000+ Medicare beneficiaries enrolled, 9,200+ participating nephrology providers.

*Source: Modarai lecture, Slide 25.*

## How this connects

| Prior concept | Connection |
|---|---|
| **Cost drivers (hss-0004)** | VBC addresses driver #3 (PCP-to-specialist ratio) by rebuilding primary care teams, and driver #5 (place of service) by shifting care from inpatient to home. |
| **Waste (hss-0004b)** | The $760-935B in annual waste is the opportunity VBC captures — preventing the hospitalizations, ED visits, and unnecessary procedures that FFS rewards. |
| **Systems thinking (hss-0005)** | VBC is a system redesign — it changes the incentive structure (macro), the care team organization (meso), and the individual encounter (micro). |
| **Teams (hss-0007)** | VBC requires team-based care. No single physician can manage a population. The physician shifts from "center of authority" to "center of care team." |
| **Population health (hss-0009)** | VBC is the payment mechanism that makes population health financially sustainable. Without VBC, prevention is a cost. With VBC, prevention is savings. |
