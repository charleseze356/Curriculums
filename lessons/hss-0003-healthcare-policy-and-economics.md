## Why this lesson exists

You cannot understand why healthcare costs are rising, why a physician's treatment choices are constrained by insurance, or why quality improvement initiatives succeed or fail without understanding the policy and economic structures underneath them.

Healthcare policy is the set of rules — laws, regulations, payment models — that govern how care is delivered, paid for, and accessed. Healthcare economics is the study of how resources are allocated within those rules: who pays, who decides, what things cost, and how incentives shape behavior.

Together, they are the operating system on which clinical practice runs. A physician who understands disease but not the economic incentives around disease cannot fully serve patients.

*Source: Skochelak et al., Health Systems Science, 2nd Ed., Ch. 4 — "Health Care Policy and Economics"; Rowan-Virtua SOM HSS II Syllabus, Objective 1 (describe healthcare structure and processes) and Objective 10 (describe drivers of healthcare costs)*

## The US healthcare system structure

The US system is distinctive among developed nations: it is **multi-payer** (many insurance sources) and **mixed public-private** (government programs coexist with private insurance and direct payment).

### Who pays?

| Payer | Who's covered | How it's funded |
|---|---|---|
| Medicare | 65+, some disabilities, ESRD | Federal payroll taxes + premiums + general revenue |
| Medicaid | Low-income individuals, varies by state | Federal + state matching funds |
| Private insurance (employer-sponsored) | ~150M Americans | Employer + employee premiums |
| Private insurance (individual market / ACA) | Self-employed, uninsured | Individual premiums + subsidies |
| Veterans Affairs (VA) | Eligible veterans | Federal funding |
| Uninsured (out-of-pocket) | ~8-10% of population | Direct payment or uncompensated care |

### Who delivers?

Care is delivered by a mix of:
- **Independent practices** — physician-owned, increasingly rare
- **Hospital-employed physicians** — the dominant model today
- **Integrated delivery systems** — e.g., Kaiser Permanente, where insurance and care delivery are unified
- **Federally Qualified Health Centers (FQHCs)** — safety-net clinics for underserved populations

### Who decides?

This is the critical question. In a single-payer system, the government decides what's covered and at what price. In the US system, **multiple actors decide**:
- **Federal government** sets Medicare rates and rules
- **State governments** regulate insurance markets and set Medicaid rules
- **Private insurers** negotiate rates with providers
- **Employers** choose which plans to offer
- **Physicians** decide what care to recommend (within the constraints of what's covered)

This fragmentation is both the system's greatest weakness (administrative complexity, inequity) and its greatest strength (multiple pathways for innovation, no single point of failure).

*Source: Skochelak et al., Health Systems Science, 2nd Ed., Ch. 4, Section I — "Overview of the US Healthcare System"*

## Payment models: the incentive engine

Payment models are the single most powerful policy lever shaping physician behavior. **How you pay physicians determines how they practice.**

### Fee-for-service (FFS)

The traditional model. Each service (office visit, procedure, test) is billed separately. More services = more revenue.

- **Incentive:** Volume. Do more, earn more.
- **Problem:** Rewards quantity over quality. Drives overutilization, unnecessary testing, and procedure-heavy medicine.
- **Where it persists:** Many specialist services, some primary care.

### Value-based payment (VBP)

Ties reimbursement to outcomes — quality metrics, cost-efficiency, patient experience. Payment is adjusted up or down based on performance.

- **Incentive:** Value. Deliver good outcomes efficiently.
- **Mechanisms:** Bundled payments (one payment for an episode of care), shared savings (keep a portion of cost reductions), capitation (per-member-per-month payment regardless of services).
- **Where it's growing:** Medicare Advantage, ACOs (Accountable Care Organizations), many commercial payer contracts.

### The shift

The US system is in a multi-decade transition from FFS toward VBP. Most physicians today operate in a **mixed model** — some FFS, some value-based. Understanding which model your future practice operates under will shape how you make clinical decisions.

*Source: Skochelak et al., Health Systems Science, 2nd Ed., Ch. 4, Section III — "Payment Models and Reimbursement"*

## Key policy concepts you need to know

### The Triple Aim → Quadruple Aim

The **Triple Aim** (Berwick et al., 2008) is the foundational policy framework for modern healthcare improvement:
1. Improve population health
2. Improve the patient experience of care
3. Reduce per-capita cost of care

The **Quadruple Aim** adds a fourth:
4. Improve the clinician experience (burnout reduction)

Every major policy initiative — ACOs, value-based payment, quality metrics — traces back to the Triple Aim. If you understand the three (now four) goals, you can decode almost any policy you encounter.

### The Affordable Care Act (ACA, 2010)

The largest healthcare policy change in 50 years. Key provisions:
- **Insurance expansion** — Medicaid expansion + health insurance marketplaces
- **Pre-existing conditions** — insurers cannot deny coverage
- **Dependent coverage** — up to age 26
- **Essential health benefits** — minimum coverage standards
- **Payment reform** — pilots for ACOs, bundled payments, value-based models

The ACA didn't create universal coverage (the US still has uninsured), but it dramatically reduced the uninsured rate and accelerated the shift toward value-based payment.

### Accountable Care Organizations (ACOs)

Groups of doctors, hospitals, and other providers who voluntarily form an entity to coordinate care for Medicare patients. If they deliver high-quality care at lower cost, they share in the savings. If costs exceed benchmarks, they may owe money back.

ACOs are the practical implementation of the Triple Aim — they create financial accountability for total cost and quality, not just individual services.

*Source: Skochelak et al., Health Systems Science, 2nd Ed., Ch. 4, Section IV — "Health Policy and the Social Contract"*

## Why this is load-bearing

Every concept in the rest of this curriculum depends on understanding policy and economics:

- **Drivers of healthcare cost** (next lesson) — you can't understand why costs rise without understanding the payment incentives that drive them
- **Value in health care** — value = quality ÷ cost. You need to understand both sides of that equation, and the payment models that shape both
- **Quality improvement** — QI initiatives succeed or fail based on whether the payment model rewards the improvement
- **Patient safety** — safety investments require resources; whether those resources are available depends on whether the payment model rewards prevention or only treatment

If you find yourself confused about why a system behaves the way it does in a later lesson, come back here. The answer is almost always in the incentives.
