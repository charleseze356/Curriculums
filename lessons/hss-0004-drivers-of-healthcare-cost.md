## Why this lesson exists

The US spends approximately **$4.5 trillion annually** on healthcare — about **17-18% of GDP**, more than any other nation. Yet US health outcomes (life expectancy, infant mortality, chronic disease management) lag behind peer nations that spend half as much.

This paradox — more spending, worse outcomes — is the central problem of healthcare economics. Understanding *why* costs are high, and *which* costs are wasteful vs. necessary, is what allows you to practice cost-conscious medicine. This is not abstract: the Aquifer High Value Care cases you're completing this week test exactly this competency.

*Source: Skochelak et al., Health Systems Science, 2nd Ed., Ch. 4, Section V — "Drivers of Healthcare Costs"; Rowan-Virtua SOM HSS II Syllabus, Objective 10 (describe the drivers of healthcare costs)*

## The scale of the problem

| Metric | US | Peer nation average (OECD) |
|---|---|---|
| Per-capita spending | ~$13,000 | ~$6,000 |
| % of GDP | ~17.3% | ~9-11% |
| Life expectancy | ~77 years | ~80-82 years |
| Infant mortality | ~5.4/1000 | ~3-4/1000 |
| Administrative costs as % of total | 15-30% | 3-5% |

The US is not delivering *more* care than peer nations by most utilization measures — fewer physician visits, fewer hospital days, similar or fewer procedures. The difference is **price** and **administrative complexity**, not volume.

*Source: Skochelak et al., Health Systems Science, 2nd Ed., Ch. 4, Section V — "Healthcare Spending in the United States"*

## The five major cost drivers

### 1. High prices (not high utilization)

The single biggest driver. US prices for drugs, procedures, and hospital services are **2-10x higher** than in peer nations for identical services:

- A standard MRI: ~$1,400 in the US vs. ~$300 in Germany
- Insulin: ~$300/vial in the US vs. ~$30 in Canada
- A normal vaginal delivery: ~$10,000+ in the US vs. ~$2,000-3,000 in peer nations

The mechanism: the US lacks centralized price negotiation (Medicare is prohibited by law from negotiating drug prices for most of its history), and each insurer negotiates separately with each provider. Providers with market power (large hospital systems) can demand high prices.

**Necessary or wasteful?** The *services* are often necessary. The *prices* are wasteful — they reflect market dysfunction, not clinical value.

### 2. Administrative complexity

The US multi-payer system requires an enormous administrative apparatus:
- Billing and coding (ICD-10, CPT codes)
- Insurance verification and prior authorization
- Claims processing and denial appeals
- Quality reporting to multiple payers with different metrics

Estimated cost: **15-30% of total healthcare spending** — hundreds of billions annually. This is pure overhead — it adds no clinical value. In single-payer systems, this cost is 3-5%.

**Necessary or wasteful?** Wasteful. It's a structural cost of the multi-payer system, not a clinical necessity.

### 3. Overutilization and defensive medicine

Physicians order tests and procedures that provide little clinical value, driven by:
- **FFS incentives** — more services = more revenue
- **Defensive medicine** — fear of litigation drives unnecessary testing "just to be sure"
- **Patient expectations** — patients may demand tests they don't need
- **System fragmentation** — duplicate testing when records aren't shared

Examples: imaging for uncomplicated back pain (guidelines say don't, but it happens constantly), antibiotics for viral infections, pre-op testing for low-risk procedures.

**Necessary or wasteful?** Wasteful. This is the target of "low-value care" reduction campaigns.

### 4. Drug pricing

The US pays significantly more for prescription drugs than any other nation:
- No centralized price negotiation (until recent IRA provisions)
- Patent extensions and "evergreening" strategies
- Direct-to-consumer advertising (only the US and New Zealand allow this)
- Pharmacy benefit managers (PBMs) with opaque rebate structures

**Necessary or wasteful?** Mixed. Drug innovation is necessary and expensive. But US prices for identical drugs are dramatically higher than peer nations — the *price differential* is wasteful.

### 5. End-of-life care

Approximately **25-30% of Medicare spending** goes to the last year of life, much of it in the final 6 months. This includes:
- Aggressive interventions (ICU stays, chemotherapy) for patients with terminal illness
- Repeated hospitalizations when palliative or hospice care would be more appropriate
- Care that extends suffering without extending meaningful life

**Necessary or wasteful?** Mixed. Appropriate end-of-life care (hospice, palliative care, advance care planning) is necessary and often *reduces* total cost while improving quality. Aggressive interventions that don't improve outcomes are wasteful.

*Source: Skochelak et al., Health Systems Science, 2nd Ed., Ch. 4, Section V — "Drivers of Healthcare Costs"; Berwick & Hackbarth, "Eliminating Waste in US Health Care" (JAMA, 2012)*

## High-value care: the response to cost drivers

**High-value care** is care that improves outcomes relative to cost. It's the practical application of cost analysis — not "spend less" but "spend better."

The framework:

| | High benefit | Low benefit |
|---|---|---|
| **High cost** | High value (specialty surgery, ICU for reversible conditions) | **Low value** (avoid — this is the target) |
| **Low cost** | High value (vaccines, generic medications, lifestyle counseling) | Reasonable (may still be worth doing for reassurance) |

The **Choosing Wisely** campaign (ABIM Foundation) asks specialty societies to publish lists of "Things Physicians and Patients Should Question" — specific low-value services to reduce. Examples:
- Don't image uncomplicated low back pain in the first 6 weeks
- Don't prescribe antibiotics for viral upper respiratory infections
- Don't order annual ECGs on asymptomatic, low-risk patients

This is what the Aquifer High Value Care cases test: can you identify which interventions provide benefit relative to cost, and which are low-value?

*Source: Skochelak et al., Health Systems Science, 2nd Ed., Ch. 5 — "Value in Health Care"; Choosing Wisely campaign (ABIM Foundation)*

## The systems thinking connection

Here's where this connects to the next chapter. Cost drivers are not isolated problems — they are **system outputs**:

- High prices → output of a market without price negotiation
- Administrative complexity → output of a multi-payer system
- Overutilization → output of FFS incentives + liability fear
- Drug pricing → output of regulatory structure + market power
- End-of-life care → output of a system that rewards intervention over conversation

**You cannot fix cost drivers by telling physicians to "be cost-conscious."** The system's incentives push toward high cost. Fixing cost requires changing the system — payment reform, price transparency, administrative simplification, and better end-of-life care models.

This is why systems thinking (the next chapter) is the linking domain. You need it to see that cost is a system output, not just a sum of individual decisions.
