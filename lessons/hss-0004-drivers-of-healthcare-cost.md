## Why this lesson exists

The US spends approximately **$4.5+ trillion annually** on healthcare — more than any other nation on earth. Yet across chronic disease prevalence, mortality, avoidable hospitalizations, life expectancy, and equity, the US ranks **bottom-tier globally** among high-income nations.

This paradox — highest spending, worst outcomes — is not abstract. Dr. Berman's lecture identifies **eight specific drivers** that explain where the money goes and why. Understanding these drivers is what allows you to practice cost-conscious medicine and is directly tested in the HSS II Quiz 1 and Aquifer High Value Care cases.

*Source: Berman EJ. "Drivers of Healthcare Cost." Rowan-Virtua SOM HSS II Lecture, June 2026. Slides 4–32.*

## The eight drivers

Dr. Berman identifies eight drivers of healthcare cost. They are not independent — they feed each other. Social determinants drive chronic disease; chronic disease drives pharmacy costs; pharmacy costs drive place-of-service decisions; all of it drives technology and imaging use. The system is interconnected.

| # | Driver | Category |
|---|--------|----------|
| 1 | Chronic care / aging population | Population health |
| 2 | Reversible illness (obesity, smoking, medication non-adherence) | Population health + behavior |
| 3 | Proportion of PCPs to specialists | System structure |
| 4 | Specialty pharmacy | System structure + innovation |
| 5 | Place of service (IP vs OP vs Home vs Convenience) | System structure + physician behavior |
| 6 | Technology / Imaging | Physician behavior + system structure |
| 7 | Fraud, waste, and abuse | System structure |
| 8 | Social determinants of health | Population health |

The next lesson (hss-0004b) covers driver #7 (fraud, waste, and abuse) in depth along with how payers manage cost and what solutions exist. This lesson covers drivers #1–6 and #8.

*Source: Berman lecture, Slide 4.*

## Driver 1: Chronic care and the aging population

### The scale of chronic disease

The US population is aging, and with longer life expectancy comes greater illness burden — both physical health (PH) and behavioral health (BH). The numbers:

- **45%** of the US population (120 million people) had **at least 1 chronic medical condition**
- **50% of those** (60 million) had **multiple chronic conditions**
- **83% of Medicare beneficiaries** had 1 or more chronic conditions
- **23% of Medicare beneficiaries** had **5 or more** chronic conditions
- By 2015, an estimated **150 million Americans** would have at least 1 chronic condition

*Source: Berman lecture, Slide 6.*

### The RAND 2017 report — who actually drives spending

The RAND Corporation's 2017 report on chronic disease reveals that cost is not distributed evenly across the chronic disease population — it is **concentrated** in the most complex patients:

- **12% of the population** had 5+ chronic conditions → accounted for **41% of total expenditures**
- **40% of the population** had no chronic conditions → accounted for only **10% of total expenditures**
- People with 5+ chronic conditions spent **twice as much** on average as those with 3 or 4
- Americans with 5+ chronic conditions spent **14x more** on health services than individuals with no chronic conditions
- The majority of additional spending was due to **office visits, inpatient visits, and prescriptions**

The functional impact compounds: individuals with more chronic conditions have greater difficulties with **Activities of Daily Living (ADL), social, and cognitive functions**. People with 5+ conditions have **more than double the number of limitations** than those with 3 or 4, placing higher demands on caregivers and the healthcare system.

*Source: Berman lecture, Slides 7–9.*

### International comparison — the US ranks bottom-tier

| Metric | US Global Rank | Notes |
|--------|---------------|-------|
| Chronic disease prevalence | Bottom tier | Highest obesity & diabetes rates among high-income nations |
| Chronic disease mortality | Bottom tier | High avoidable mortality from heart disease, diabetes, COPD |
| Chronic-care spending | **Highest globally** | Largest share of national health expenditures |
| Avoidable hospitalizations | Bottom tier | Weak primary care & high cost barriers |
| Life expectancy impact | Bottom tier | Chronic disease is the primary driver of lower US life expectancy |
| Equity in chronic disease outcomes | Bottom tier | Large racial & geographic disparities |

*Source: Berman lecture, Slide 10.*

**The relationship:** The US doesn't just have more chronic disease — it manages it worse. Weak primary care and cost barriers lead to avoidable hospitalizations, which are the most expensive way to manage chronic disease. The spending is high because the system reacts to crises instead of preventing them.

## Driver 2: Reversible illness — obesity, smoking, and medication non-adherence

These are called "reversible" because they are modifiable through behavior change, policy intervention, or clinical management. They are the drivers where physician engagement has the most direct leverage.

### Obesity — "a common, serious, and costly disease"

- **Prevalence: 42.4%** in 2017–2018, up from 30.5% in 1999–2000
- **Severe obesity: 9.2%**, up from 4.7% in the same period
- Now affects **1 in 5 (20%) children, adolescents, and adults** in the US
- Related conditions: **heart disease, stroke, type 2 diabetes, certain cancers** — leading causes of preventable, premature death
- Estimated annual medical cost: **$147 billion** (2008 dollars)
- Medical cost for people with obesity: **$1,429 higher** than those of normal weight

*Source: Berman lecture, Slide 11. Data: CDC.*

**International comparison:** The US ranks **#1–2 globally** in adult obesity prevalence and **#1 among high-income nations** in childhood obesity. Obesity-related healthcare spending is the **highest globally**. Obesity is a major driver of lower US life expectancy, with large racial and geographic disparities in outcomes.

*Source: Berman lecture, Slide 14.*

### Smoking — $300 billion per year

- Total cost: **more than $300 billion each year**
- Direct medical care for adults: **nearly $170 billion**
- Lost productivity: **more than $156 billion**, including $5.6 billion from secondhand smoke exposure

*Source: Berman lecture, Slide 15. Data: CDC.*

**The price lever:** Increasing the price of tobacco products is the **single most effective way to reduce consumption**. A **10% increase in price** reduces overall cigarette consumption by **3–5%**. Youth and young adults are **2–3x more responsive** to price increases than adults. Lower-income populations are more likely to increase quit attempts or smoke fewer cigarettes in response to price increases — though successful cessation may not be consistent across all racial/ethnic groups.

*Source: Berman lecture, Slide 16.*

**International comparison:** While adult smoking prevalence in the US is mid-range (lower than many OECD peers), **youth vaping prevalence is #1 among high-income nations**. Adult vaping prevalence is also top-tier. Smoking-related mortality and healthcare spending remain among the highest globally.

*Source: Berman lecture, Slide 17.*

### Medication non-adherence

Medication non-adherence is identified as a reversible driver of cost — patients not taking medications as prescribed leads to disease progression, complications, and avoidable hospitalizations. This connects directly to the chronic disease burden: a patient with 5+ chronic conditions who is non-adherent to even a subset of their medications will cycle through emergency visits and hospitalizations that are far more expensive than the medications themselves.

*Source: Berman lecture, Slides 19–20.*

## Driver 3: Proportion of PCPs to specialists

There is an **inverse relationship between the number of generalists (PCPs) and the cost of healthcare**. This is visible in international comparisons: countries with a higher proportion of PCPs to specialists spend less per capita on healthcare and achieve better outcomes.

The US has a lower PCP-to-specialist ratio than peer nations. This drives higher costs because:

- Specialists order more procedures and tests
- Specialists use more technology and imaging
- Specialist encounters cost more per visit
- Fragmentation of care across multiple specialists leads to duplicated services and poor care coordination

**The mechanism:** Primary care is the coordination layer. When it's weak, patients enter the system through the most expensive doors (ED, specialist referrals, inpatient) instead of the cheapest (PCP office visit, preventive care). Strengthening primary care is identified as a key lever for cost reduction.

*Source: Berman lecture, Slide 18. Reference: OECD Health Data — "Inverse relationship between number of generalists and cost of health care."*

## Driver 4: Specialty pharmacy

### Medication trends and projected cost shifts

| Category | Current Trend | Projected Trend | Cost Impact |
|----------|--------------|-----------------|-------------|
| Generics | Flat or declining prices | Slow growth | Keeps costs down but shrinking influence. ~90% of prescriptions but <20% of spend. May fall to 15–18% of total drug spend by 2030. |
| Brand-name drugs | Rising prices | Continued growth | Major driver of spending increases. Will represent ~60% of total drug spending by 2030. |
| Specialty drugs | Rapid growth | Explosive growth | Dominant cost driver. May represent ~70% of total spending by 2035. |
| Biosimilars | Increasing uptake | Strong growth | Will reduce some biologic costs but not enough to offset specialty growth. |

*Source: Berman lecture, Slide 22.*

### Biologics and biosimilars

**Biologics** are medications made from materials from living organisms — including many specialty medications, vaccines, and insulins. **Biosimilars** are essentially generic versions of biologic drugs.

Key distinctions from generic drugs:

- **No clinically meaningful differences** between a biologic and its biosimilar
- Biosimilars **cannot be substituted by a pharmacist** unless the FDA deems them interchangeable
- Biologics have a **12-year exclusivity period** (vs. the normal 5 years for brand drugs) — meaning it takes longer for a cheaper biosimilar to launch and compete
- While generic medications are **80–85% cheaper** than brand-name counterparts, biosimilars are only **10–37% cheaper** than biologics
- The most recently approved biosimilars are generally the cheapest

*Source: Berman lecture, Slides 23–24.*

### Top medication cost drivers

| Category | Examples | Cost Dynamics | Key Drivers of Growth |
|----------|---------|---------------|----------------------|
| Oncology | Immunotherapies, targeted therapies, CAR-T | Very high annual costs ($100K+ per patient), expanding indications | Aging population, earlier use of high-cost agents, combination regimens, longer treatment durations |
| Autoimmune & Inflammatory | Biologics for RA, psoriasis, IBD, severe asthma | Chronic, long-term therapy ($50K–$80K/year) | Rising prevalence, earlier biologic initiation, multiple agents per patient, step-therapy failures |
| Gene & Cell Therapies | Gene therapies for rare diseases, CAR-T, emerging cell therapies | Ultra-high one-time costs ($1M–$3M+ per treatment) | New approvals, expanded indications, shift toward curative but extremely expensive interventions |
| Rare & Orphan Diseases | Enzyme replacement, niche biologics | High per-patient cost with small populations | More orphan drug approvals, strong pricing power, limited competition |
| Metabolic & Cardiovascular | GLP-1s, PCSK9 inhibitors, novel lipid and HF drugs | Rapid uptake and high monthly costs | Obesity epidemic, cardiometabolic focus, expanded use beyond highest-risk patients |

*Source: Berman lecture, Slide 25.*

### The US vs. the rest of the world

Americans spent more on the top 20 most-prescribed medications than **the rest of the world combined** (Public Citizen analysis, 2020 data):

- For **17 of the 20** top-selling drugs, pharmaceutical companies made more revenue from US sales than from every other country put together
- For **11 of the top 20**, US sales revenue was **double** that from the rest of the world combined
- The 20 top-selling drugs brought in **$158 billion globally**; US sales made up **$101 billion (64%)**

Specific examples:
- Gilead's HIV drug **Biktarvy**: $6.1B US vs. $1.2B rest of world (5x)
- AbbVie's **Humira** (autoimmune): $16B US vs. $4B rest of world (4x)
- Eli Lilly's **Trulicity** (diabetes): $3.8B US vs. $1.2B rest of world (3x)

*Source: Berman lecture, Slides 26–27. Original: Forbes/Public Citizen, Sept 2021.*

As of 2026, the US has **more than 45 FDA-approved biosimilars** across oncology, autoimmune disease, diabetes, ophthalmology, and bone health. The largest clusters are for adalimumab (Humira), trastuzumab (Herceptin), bevacizumab (Avastin), and pegfilgrastim (Neulasta).

*Source: Berman lecture, Slide 28.*

## Driver 5: Place of service

Where care is delivered drives what it costs. The same service costs dramatically different amounts depending on the setting:

- **Inpatient (IP)** — most expensive; includes facility fees, 24/7 staffing, overhead
- **Outpatient (OP)** — less expensive; but hospital-owned outpatient facilities can charge facility fees
- **Home** — least expensive for appropriate services; telemedicine enables this
- **Convenience** — retail clinics, urgent care; mid-range cost, but when used instead of the ED for non-emergent issues, represents significant savings

The cost differential between settings is enormous. An unnecessary ED visit costs the system far more than the same complaint managed in a PCP office or via telemedicine. This driver connects directly to the $47 billion in unnecessary ED visits covered in the next lesson.

*Source: Berman lecture, Slides 4, 33, 45.*

## Driver 6: Technology and imaging

Advanced imaging (CT, MRI, PET) is a major cost driver, with significant waste:

- AHIP estimates **$30 billion per year** is "wasted" on advanced imaging
- **20–50% of advanced diagnostic procedures were unhelpful** for diagnosis
- The American College of Radiology estimates **20–30% of advanced imaging studies** are not the appropriate study, do not add anything to the diagnosis, or are repeated too soon

Medicare alone spends **$3.6 billion annually** on unnecessary imaging; across all US payers, the cost is approximately **$12 billion**.

*Source: Berman lecture, Slides 35–36.*

## Driver 8: Social determinants of health

**Social determinants of health (SDOH)** are defined by the WHO as "the conditions in which people are born, grow, work, live, and age, and the wider set of forces and systems shaping the conditions of daily life."

Dr. Berman frames SDOH through **Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs** — if basic needs aren't met, health cannot be prioritized:

- **Poverty**
- **Food insecurity**
- **Homelessness**
- **Violence**
- **Health illiteracy**

*Source: Berman lecture, Slide 5.*

**The systems connection:** SDOH is not a separate driver — it is the **upstream cause** of drivers #1 and #2. Poverty drives food insecurity, which drives obesity and diabetes. Homelessness drives ED utilization. Health illiteracy drives medication non-adherence and delayed care. You cannot reduce chronic disease costs without addressing the social conditions that produce chronic disease.

This is why the lecture identifies "reversing imbalances in distribution of services" and addressing SDOH as key physician responsibilities — not as charity, but as cost strategy.

## Where the money goes: 2024 spending breakdown

Understanding the drivers requires understanding where dollars actually land.

### By spending category (2024)

| Spending Category | 2024 Spending | Share of Total | Growth Rate |
|-------------------|--------------|----------------|-------------|
| Hospital Care | $1.63T | 31% | 8.9% |
| Physician & Clinical Services | $1.11T | 21% | 8.1% |
| Retail Prescription Drugs | $467B | 9% | 7.9% |
| Out-of-Pocket Spending | $556.6B | 11% | 5.9% |
| Other Third-Party Payers & Programs | $590.5B | 11% | –7.0% |
| Other Health, Residential & Personal Care | ~$590B | 11% | –7.0% |

*Source: Berman lecture, Slide 31.*

### By source of spending (2024)

| Payer Category | Share | Why It's Significant |
|----------------|-------|---------------------|
| Federal Government (Medicare, federal Medicaid, VA, TRICARE, IHS, ACA subsidies) | 31% | Largest single payer; aging population and chronic disease drive rapid growth |
| Households (out-of-pocket, premiums) | 28% | High deductibles and drug prices place financial burden on families; major driver of medical debt |
| Private Businesses (employer premium contributions) | 18% | Employer-sponsored insurance covers ~150M Americans; rising premiums affect wages and hiring |
| State & Local Governments (state Medicaid, employee benefits, public health) | 16% | Medicaid is jointly funded; states bear significant responsibility for safety-net care |
| Other Private Revenues (philanthropy, workers' comp, investment income) | 6% | Fills gaps; supports safety-net providers and nonprofit systems |

*Source: Berman lecture, Slide 32.*

**The relationship:** The "ultimate payer is the consumer" — even when the federal government or employers write the check, the money comes from taxes, wages, and premiums. Households bear 28% directly and indirectly fund the rest through taxes and reduced wages. This is why healthcare affordability is not just a patient problem — it's an economic problem that suppresses wages and drives medical debt.

## The interconnection: why these drivers can't be solved in isolation

The eight drivers form a system, not a list:

- **SDOH** (driver 8) → produces **chronic disease** (driver 1) and **reversible illness** (driver 2)
- Chronic disease → drives **specialty pharmacy** costs (driver 4) and **place-of-service** utilization (driver 5)
- Weak **PCP ratio** (driver 3) → means patients enter through expensive doors (ED, specialist) instead of primary care → drives **technology/imaging** use (driver 6) and **fraud/waste/abuse** (driver 7)
- All of it → feeds **hospital care** (31% of spending) and **physician services** (21%)

This is why Dr. Berman's "What Does This Mean for You?" section (Slide 53) doesn't ask physicians to just "be cost-conscious" — it asks them to **reverse imbalances in the distribution of services**, address SDOH, practice to the full extent of licensure, and embrace integrated care management. The drivers are system outputs; fixing them requires system change.
